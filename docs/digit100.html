Corpus Analysis

The three texts I chose to compare were Biden, Trump, and Obama's inaugural speeches. Using Voyant, I discovered certain terms that each president used very often.

In all three speeches, each president used words focused on America and the people. There is very little difference in their word clouds, however, there are some words like “democracy” that are highlighted quite often in Biden’s speech and “nation” in Obama’s speech.

I find it interesting that Biden’s speech has a total of 2,397 words with an average of 11.5 words per sentence and Trump’s has 1,281 and an average of 16.2. Obama’s speech has 2,413 words and 21.4 average words per sentence. Trump’s speech might be more concise than Biden’s and Obama’s, but the sentences have varying structures. Obama has the biggest average and the most words. The most frequent words used in Trump’s speech is America, people, American, new, and nation. In Biden’s speech is America, nation, story, people, and democracy. In Obama’s speech is nation, new, America, people, and—oddly enough—let.

It is interesting to see the distribution of “my whole soul is” when using an N-gram of 4. Biden uses this phrase often and every time he follows with “in” meaning there more context to the phrase usage. When looking at 3 N-grams, he often uses the phrase “here we stand.” When looking in the text, he used this phrase very close to each other, possibly trying to get a point across.

Within Trump’s speech, with a 3 N-gram search phrases came up like, “we will bring” and “bring back our.” These phrases were commonly used together. In a 4 N-gram search, “we will make America” was used four times throughout the speech. This phrase was used in the same sentence.

Looking at the Obama speech, there wasn’t many sayings with a very high frequency. The frequency ranges were from 1-4 for an N-gram of 3 and 1-2 for an N-gram of four. Some of Obama’s phrases were “a new era of” and “men and women.” Obama also spread out his use of these phrases in his speech.

Conclusion:

After viewing both N-grams and KWICs of Trump, Biden, and Obama, it seems that they both used repetitive phrases to get their points across. However, Trump’s phrases seem to focus on America, but Obama and Biden’s phrases seem more personal. It was interesting to see the contrast in how words are articulated and how each of them used repetitive phrases to get their point across.

Game Analysis - Wolfenstein 3D

In this game, the doors and wall created that illusion that I was exploring a world. The graphics are pretty simple, it was very pixelated but features detailed items like paintings.

The settings were simple as the rooms were pretty much the same, just with different features. You can explore until you get to the end of the map or the door with the switch. If you went to the next level it would look different.

You control a soldier in this game. In the middle of the screen there is an image of the soldiers face and he looks side to side with an investigative look. When he gets hurt, he has a wounded expression. With the NPC characters, you shoot them. That is the goal.

As a game player, you are challenged by being met with Nazis and German Shepards. This game doesn’t really have an investigative gameplay to it, but it does allow you to level up as you surpass each room.

As far as objects, players interact with food, health items, treasure, and doors. Food is only for health and treasure is to collect more points.

The music is upbeat and slower in some areas of it. I think this has to do with the fact that you’re trying to beat the levels as fast as possible. The other sounds in the game just have to do with gun shots and the grunts of the characters that warn you if there is someone around or the character is hurt.

The game comments on itself in a “meta” way in the beginning when selecting what type of difficult you would like. For example, for the easier gameplay, it has, “Can I play, Daddy?” The game is making fun of each difficulty levels.

There wasn’t much commentary on the realities at the time in this game, however, the basis of the game is to shoot Nazis. This game might have been politically motivated.

This game is an old game. I don't believe it could necessarily develop in the future. If we were developing it back in the day, however, things that could be added include more weapon and enemy variety and improved graphics.

Reflection

"Wolfenstein 3D," a first-person shooter from 1992, greatly added to the FPS genre. Set in World War II, its environment and attention to detail in recreating Nazi strongholds added depth to the gameplay. While focusing on the player's avatar, B.J. Blazkowicz, the game's enemies, Nazis, serve as powerful enemies.

Objects within the game world, such as weapons, food, and secret treasures, showcase gameplay and reinforce themes of resistance and adventure. Its maze-like level design encourages exploration and rewards players with resources.

However, "Wolfenstein 3D" is not without flaws. Its simple narrative and lack of character development may leave some players wanting more depth. Additionally, its portrayal of violence, especially Nazi initiatives, may unsettle some players.

As an art form, "Wolfenstein 3D" offers a unique perspective into the potential of interactive FPS's to evoke emotion and provoke thought. Its influence on gaming cannot be overshawdowed, making it a classic worth exploring for its historical significance and innovative gameplay mechanics.
